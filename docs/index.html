1. Reflection Equations

Reflection describes how waves behave when encountering a boundary between two media.

Geometric Optics

The law of reflection states that the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection, measured from the normal:

$$\theta_i = \theta_r$$

Fresnel Equations (Physical Optics)

Used to calculate amplitude reflection coefficients (\(r\)) for different wave polarizations:

$$r_s = \frac{n_1 \cos\theta_i - n_2 \cos\theta_t}{n_1 \cos\theta_i + n_2 \cos\theta_t}$$ $$r_p = \frac{n_2 \cos\theta_i - n_1 \cos\theta_t}{n_2 \cos\theta_i + n_1 \cos\theta_t}$$

Reflectance (Power): \( R = |r|^2 \)

Normal Incidence (\(\theta_i = 0\))

At direct incidence, the equations simplify. This is standard for basic link budgets.

Metric Equation
Reflection Coefficient (\(r\)) \( \frac{n_2 - n_1}{n_2 + n_1} \)
Reflectance (\(R\)) \( \left( \frac{n_2 - n_1}{n_2 + n_1} \right)^2 \)
Transmission (\(T\)) \( 1 - R \)

統 User Query Note

Q: Reflection in Coordinate Geometry?

A: To reflect a vector \(\vec{v}\) across a plane with unit normal \(\vec{n}\): $$\vec{v}_{refl} = \vec{v} - 2(\vec{v} \cdot \vec{n})\vec{n}$$

2. Refractive Index (\(n\))

A dimensionless number describing how fast a wave travels through a medium compared to a vacuum.

$$n = \frac{c}{v}$$
  • Air: \(n \approx 1.0003\). Waves travel at ~99.97% of \(c\).
  • Water: Optical frequencies \(n \approx 1.33\). RF frequencies have high effective index (\(\approx 9\)) and massive absorption.

3. Advanced Wave Physics

統 User Query Note

Q: What is dielectric constant, relative permittivity?

A: They are the same. It measures resistance to electric field formation compared to a vacuum. $$\varepsilon_r = \frac{\tilde{\varepsilon}}{\varepsilon_0}$$ The complex refractive index is the square root of relative permittivity: \(\tilde{n} = \sqrt{\varepsilon_r}\).

統 User Query Note

Q: What do you mean water molecules are highly polar?

A: Water has an uneven charge distribution (permanent dipole moment \(\mathbf{p}\)). When high-frequency EM fields pass through, they exert torque (\(\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{p} \times \mathbf{E}\)). At RF/microwave frequencies, water molecules can't rotate fast enough to keep up, creating "molecular friction" that turns wave energy into heat.

統 User Query Note

Q: What is absorption factor?

A: Also known as the attenuation coefficient (\(\alpha\)). It dictates power loss over distance \(z\) via the Beer-Lambert Law: \( I(z) = I_0 e^{-\alpha z} \).
It is tied to the imaginary part of the refractive index: \( \alpha = \frac{4\pi \kappa}{\lambda_0} \).

統 User Query Note

Q: What do you mean opaque to electromagnetic fields (Metals)?

A: Metals have near-infinite conductivity (\(\sigma \to \infty\)). Free electrons instantly rearrange to cancel out the incoming electric field (\(\mathbf{E}_{inside} = 0\)). Since the wave cannot enter, it is perfectly reflected.

Complex Refractive Index & Amplitude Decay

Metals and lossy materials use a complex refractive index: \( \tilde{n} = n + i\kappa \)

  • \(n\): Real part (phase velocity).
  • \(\kappa\): Extinction coefficient (decay/loss).

If we substitute \(\tilde{n}\) into the plane wave equation \( E(z,t) = E_0 e^{i(kz - \omega t)} \):

$$k = (n + i\kappa)\frac{\omega}{c}$$ $$E(z,t) = E_0 e^{i\left((n + i\kappa)\frac{\omega}{c}z - \omega t\right)}$$

Distributing the \(i\) (where \(i^2 = -1\)) yields:

$$E(z,t) = \underbrace{E_0 e^{-\kappa\frac{\omega}{c}z}}_{\text{Decay Envelope}} \cdot \underbrace{e^{i\left(n\frac{\omega}{c}z - \omega t\right)}}_{\text{Oscillating Phase}}$$

Final Amplitude Equation:

$$E_{amplitude}(z) = E_0 e^{-\kappa\frac{\omega}{c}z}$$

4. Derivation of Fresnel Equations

Derived using Maxwell's Boundary Conditions for non-magnetic media (\(\mu_1 = \mu_2 = \mu_0\)):

  1. The component of the electric field (\(\mathbf{E}\)) tangent to the boundary must be continuous.
  2. The component of the magnetic field (\(\mathbf{H}\)) tangent to the boundary must be continuous.

We use the relationship \(H \propto nE\). Reflection (\(r\)) and transmission (\(t\)) coefficients are ratios of electric field amplitudes: \(r = \frac{E_r}{E_i}\) and \(t = \frac{E_t}{E_i}\).

s-Polarization (Transverse Electric)

\(\mathbf{E}\) is perpendicular to the plane of incidence (fully tangent to the boundary).

Step 1: E-field continuity (tangent)

$$E_{is} + E_{rs} = E_{ts} \implies 1 + r_s = t_s \quad \text{--- (Eq 1)}$$

Step 2: H-field continuity (tangent projection requires cosine)

$$n_1 E_{is} \cos\theta_i - n_1 E_{rs} \cos\theta_i = n_2 E_{ts} \cos\theta_t$$ $$n_1 \cos\theta_i (1 - r_s) = n_2 \cos\theta_t t_s \quad \text{--- (Eq 2)}$$

Step 3: Substitute Eq 1 into Eq 2 and solve

$$n_1 \cos\theta_i (1 - r_s) = n_2 \cos\theta_t (1 + r_s)$$ $$r_s = \frac{n_1 \cos\theta_i - n_2 \cos\theta_t}{n_1 \cos\theta_i + n_2 \cos\theta_t}$$

p-Polarization (Transverse Magnetic)

\(\mathbf{H}\) is fully tangent, while \(\mathbf{E}\) lies in the plane of incidence.

Step 1: H-field continuity (tangent)

$$n_1 E_{ip} - n_1 E_{rp} = n_2 E_{tp} \implies n_1 (1 - r_p) = n_2 t_p \quad \text{--- (Eq 3)}$$

Step 2: E-field continuity (tangent projection requires cosine)

$$E_{ip} \cos\theta_i + E_{rp} \cos\theta_r = E_{tp} \cos\theta_t \implies \cos\theta_i (1 + r_p) = t_p \cos\theta_t \quad \text{--- (Eq 4)}$$

Step 3: Isolate \(t_p\) in Eq 3, substitute into Eq 4 and solve

$$\cos\theta_i (1 + r_p) = \frac{n_1}{n_2} (1 - r_p) \cos\theta_t$$ $$r_p = \frac{n_2 \cos\theta_i - n_1 \cos\theta_t}{n_2 \cos\theta_i + n_1 \cos\theta_t}$$

5. Irradiance & Energy Conservation

Irradiance (\(I\)) is the electromagnetic power per unit area (\(\text{W/m}^2\)). \(I \propto n|E|^2\).

Reflectance (\(R\)) and Transmittance (\(T\))

$$R = \frac{I_r}{I_i} = |r|^2$$ $$T = \frac{I_t \cos\theta_t}{I_i \cos\theta_i} = \left( \frac{n_2 \cos\theta_t}{n_1 \cos\theta_i} \right) |t|^2$$

統 Why does T have an extra scaling factor?

1. Impedance Change (\(n_2/n_1\)): The wave enters a new medium. Energy density is tied to the material's permittivity. Even with the same E-field amplitude, the actual power carried is different in a denser/lighter material.

2. Beam Cross-Section Change (\(\cos\theta_t/\cos\theta_i\)): Refraction changes the angle of propagation, which geometrically stretches or compresses the physical width of the beam. Since Irradiance is Power/Area, this change in area must be accounted for.

Proof of Energy Conservation (\(R + T = 1\))

To prove that no energy is lost, we must show that \(|r|^2 + |t|^2 \times \text{scale} = 1\).

s-Polarization Proof

Let \(a = n_1 \cos\theta_i\) and \(b = n_2 \cos\theta_t\).

$$r_s = \frac{a - b}{a + b}, \quad t_s = \frac{2a}{a + b}, \quad \text{scale} = \frac{b}{a}$$ $$R_s + T_s = \left(\frac{a - b}{a + b}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{2a}{a + b}\right)^2 \left(\frac{b}{a}\right)$$ $$= \frac{(a - b)^2}{(a + b)^2} + \frac{4a^2 b}{a(a + b)^2} = \frac{(a - b)^2 + 4ab}{(a + b)^2}$$ $$= \frac{a^2 - 2ab + b^2 + 4ab}{(a + b)^2} = \frac{(a + b)^2}{(a + b)^2} = 1$$

p-Polarization Proof

Let \(c = n_2 \cos\theta_i\) and \(d = n_1 \cos\theta_t\).

$$r_p = \frac{c - d}{c + d}, \quad t_p = \frac{2 n_1 \cos\theta_i}{c + d}, \quad \text{scale} = \frac{n_2 \cos\theta_t}{n_1 \cos\theta_i}$$ $$R_p + T_p = \left(\frac{c - d}{c + d}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{2 n_1 \cos\theta_i}{c + d}\right)^2 \left(\frac{n_2 \cos\theta_t}{n_1 \cos\theta_i}\right)$$ $$= \frac{(c - d)^2}{(c + d)^2} + \frac{4 (n_1 \cos\theta_i)(n_2 \cos\theta_t)}{(c + d)^2}$$

Notice that \((n_1 \cos\theta_i)(n_2 \cos\theta_t) = (n_2 \cos\theta_i)(n_1 \cos\theta_t) = cd\).

$$= \frac{(c - d)^2 + 4cd}{(c + d)^2} = \frac{c^2 - 2cd + d^2 + 4cd}{(c + d)^2} = \frac{(c + d)^2}{(c + d)^2} = 1$$
