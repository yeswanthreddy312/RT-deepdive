11. Wavefront Mechanics & The Ray Abstraction
Core Concept: A ray is not a physical entity — it is a mathematical construct drawn normal to the wavefront surface, tracking the local direction of Poynting energy flow (\(\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}\)).

Definition: What is a Wavefront?

A wavefront is a locus of points in space sharing an identical phase of an electromagnetic wave — the co-phasal surface where the electric field simultaneously reaches its maximum amplitude. Mathematically, this surface is defined by:

$$\omega t - \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{r} = \text{constant}$$

where \(\omega\) is the angular frequency, \(\mathbf{k}\) is the wave vector, and \(\mathbf{r}\) is the position vector in 3D space.

The Ray Proxy

In geometric ray tracing, a ray is purely a mathematical construct drawn strictly normal (perpendicular) to the physical wavefront surface. Its direction tracks the local vector of Poynting energy flow — the direction in which electromagnetic energy is transported:

$$\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}$$

The ray carries no intrinsic physical width or energy of its own. It is an abstraction of the local propagation direction of the wavefront it represents.

The Intuition: What is a Wavefront?

Imagine freezing time while an antenna is transmitting. The electromagnetic wave is frozen in space. A wavefront is an imaginary 3D surface that connects all the points of the wave that are at the exact same phase — for example, all the peaks of the electric field.

Think of dropping a pebble into a perfectly still pond. The expanding, circular ripple traveling outward is a wavefront. Every drop of water on the peak of that specific ripple is in the exact same phase of its up-and-down cycle. For a 5G/6G signal, instead of a 2D circle on a pond, it is a 3D expanding balloon of electromagnetic energy.

The Bridge to Ray Tracing

Rays do not physically exist. A "ray" is purely a mathematical abstraction — an arrow drawn strictly perpendicular (normal) to the wavefront, indicating the direction the wave's energy is traveling. Field Transformations: Near-Field vs. Far-Field

Near-Field (Spherical Wavefronts)

Point sources emit expanding spherical wavefront surfaces. Rays point radially outward (\(\nabla \times \hat{\mathbf{r}} = 0\)). Power density decays via the Inverse Square Law as the fixed total power spreads over an ever-growing spherical surface area:

$$P_d \propto \frac{1}{r^2}$$

Far-Field (Plane Wavefronts)

At vast distances relative to wavelength — specifically where \(r \gg \frac{2D^2}{\lambda}\) (the Fraunhofer distance, with \(D\) being the aperture size) — a localized slice of the spherical wavefront becomes effectively flat. Wavefront surfaces become perfectly parallel planes; the tracking ray vectors align into uniform, non-diverging parallel sets. This is the plane wave approximation that underlies all classical geometric ray tracing.

12. High-Frequency Diffraction (Huygens’ Principle & UTD)
Core Concept: Diffraction bends signal power around sharp obstacles into geometric shadow zones. Computational ray tracers replace full wave integration with the Uniform Theory of Diffraction (UTD) — a localized coefficient and a cone of secondary diffracted rays per edge hit.

Huygens-Fresnel Principle

Every unobstructed point on a primary wavefront acts as a secondary, localized source of spherical wavelets. The forward envelope of all these wavelets synthesizes the next wavefront position. When a wavefront encounters a sharp obstacle edge (e.g., a building corner), it geometrically blocks some secondary sources. The remaining unblocked wavelets continue to expand radially — including inward behind the geometric boundary — bending signal power directly into the "shadow zone" that classical geometric optics would predict to be completely dark.

Frequency Scaling of Diffraction Efficiency

Wavelength \(\lambda\) directly dictates how efficiently edges bend signals:

  • Low frequencies (~700 MHz, \(\lambda \approx 43\text{ cm}\)): Wavelengths are comparable to or larger than urban macro-structures. Diffraction is smooth and efficient — signals bend readily around buildings and terrain obstacles.
  • Next-gen high frequencies (6G sub-THz ~100 GHz, \(\lambda \approx 3\text{ mm}\)): Wavelengths become tiny relative to all structural features. Diffraction efficiency drops to near-zero, casting deep, knife-edge radio shadow zones with sharply defined boundaries and severe signal loss behind them.

Computational Shortcut: Uniform Theory of Diffraction (UTD)

Full wave integration across the aperture is computationally intractable at ray-tracing scale. The UTD provides an efficient geometric approximation by treating diffraction as a local phenomenon at the edge:

  1. Model the physical obstacle edge as a 3D geometric line vector in the scene.
  2. When an incident ray intersects this edge line, compute a localized diffraction coefficient tensor \(\mathbf{D}\) (dependent on geometry, frequency, polarization, and material).
  3. Project a continuous spatial Keller Cone of secondary diffracted rays around the edge axis — all emerging at the same polar angle from the edge as the incident ray, but spread across all azimuthal directions, wrapping around the obstacle corner profile.

This collapses the full aperture integral to a single local calculation per edge intersection, making diffraction tractable in large-scale urban ray tracing simulations.

The Intuition: Wavelength vs. The Obstacle

Diffraction doesn't just happen because a wave is "big" or "small." It happens based on the ratio of the wavelength to the sharpness of the edge.

  • Low Frequency / Long Wavelength (4G at ~700 MHz, \(\lambda \approx 43\text{ cm}\)): The wave is physically thick. When it hits a sharp building edge, the Huygens wavelets are large enough to spill over the edge, wrapping signal energy into the shadow zone. 4G penetrates basements and turns corners because of this efficient diffraction.
  • High Frequency / Short Wavelength (6G at ~100 GHz, \(\lambda \approx 3\text{ mm}\)): At this scale, the building edge acts like a massive, infinite wall. The wave shoots straight past, leaving a deep, sharp radio shadow. 6G must compensate entirely via reflections and dense small-cell deployments.

The Physics: Why Wavelength Kills the Shadow Signal

Every Huygens wavelet spreads out perfectly spherically regardless of frequency. The shadow is not caused by a lack of spread — it is caused by destructive interference driven by path length differences \(\Delta d\).

When two adjacent wavelets \(W_1\) and \(W_2\) travel into the shadow zone, \(W_2\) travels a slightly longer path. That path difference translates directly to a phase shift:

$$\Delta\phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \cdot \Delta d$$
  • 4G (\(\lambda = 43\text{ cm}\), \(\Delta d = 1.5\text{ cm}\)): \(\Delta\phi \approx 0.22\text{ rad}\). The wavelets arrive nearly in sync and add together constructively into the shadow zone.
  • 6G (\(\lambda = 3\text{ mm}\), \(\Delta d = 1.5\text{ cm}\)): \(\Delta\phi = 10\pi\text{ rad}\). The same tiny detour causes a \(1800°\) phase shift. The wavelets obliterate each other.

Q&A: The Illusion of Aligned Phase

Q: "For high frequencies, even if the wave cycled multiple times, can't it arrive back in aligned phase? After cycling \(2\pi\) it is again at the same phase, right?"

Mathematically yes, but physically no, due to two brutal realities:

1. The Spatial Barcode: Yes, there exists a precise spot where wavelets align at \(2\lambda\) — a signal spike. But moving your phone just 1.5 mm (half a 6G wavelength) shifts the path to \(2.5\lambda\). Waves are now \(180°\) out of phase — dead zone. This creates a microscopic chaotic barcode of alternating signal spikes and dead zones, spaced only millimeters apart. A moving device crosses hundreds per second, making the link unstable.

2. Phase Smearing (The Real Killer): We don't sum two wavelets — Huygens' Principle says we sum infinite wavelets along the edge. At 4G, all arrive with tiny phase offsets (0°, 2°, 5°) and add massively. At 6G, wavelets arrive completely scrambled across every phase (0°, 90°, 180°, 270°…). Summing hundreds of waves uniformly distributed across the full 360° phase circle gives zero. The interactive visualizer below demonstrates this collapse directly.

13. Surface Roughness & Diffuse Scattering (BRDF)
Core Concept: The Rayleigh Criterion determines whether a surface acts as a specular mirror or a diffuse scatterer. For millimeter-wave 6G signals, virtually all common building materials violate the smoothness threshold and shatter specular reflections into diffuse paths.

Rayleigh Roughness Criterion

A physical surface with local protuberances of height \(h\) ceases to behave as a specular mirror and acts as a diffuse wave scatterer when the following threshold is exceeded:

$$h > \frac{\lambda}{8 \cos\theta_i}$$

where \(\theta_i\) is the angle of incidence relative to the surface normal. For millimetric 6G waves (\(\lambda \approx 3\text{ mm}\)), even sub-millimeter surface variations on normal brick, concrete, and masonry elements easily violate this boundary, scattering specular energy into diffuse propagation paths throughout the environment.

BRDF Mathematical Implementations

1. Lambertian Model (Purely Diffuse)

The scattered field power \(P_s\) exiting at any reflection angle \(\theta_s\) depends only on the projection cosine to the surface normal vector — entirely decoupled from the incoming ray direction:

$$P_s(\theta_s) \propto \cos(\theta_s)$$

This is the maximum-entropy scattering model: energy is distributed uniformly across the hemisphere, weighted by the solid angle projection factor. It is physically valid for deeply rough surfaces (concrete, asphalt, bare earth) where all phase memory of the incident wave is destroyed by surface irregularities.

2. Directive Model (Combined Specular + Diffuse)

A more physically accurate model introduces an empirical Scattering Coefficient \(S \in [0, 1]\). Starting from the baseline Fresnel reflection power coefficient \(R\), the specular and diffuse power fractions are apportioned as follows:

  • Specular power fraction: \(R_{\text{specular}} = R \cdot (1 - S)\) — the Fresnel reflection attenuated by the fraction of energy lost to scattering.
  • Diffuse power fraction: \(R \cdot S\) — scales a directive spatial power lobe centered along the geometric specular reflection track, spreading energy around it.

When \(S = 0\), the model degenerates to a pure Fresnel specular mirror. When \(S = 1\), all reflected power is scattered diffusely. Empirically measured values include: glass \(S \approx 0.1\), brick \(S \approx 0.5\text{–}0.7\), rough concrete \(S \approx 0.8\).
