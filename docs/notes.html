Module: Electromagnetism & Maxwell's Laws

Source: Electromagnetism - Maxwell's Laws (Eugene Khutoryansky) & Independent Research

Video Summary: This module visually breaks down Maxwell's equations from first principles using 3D animations. It provides intuitive, geometric explanations for how electric and magnetic fields interact, why permanent magnets work at the atomic level (spin vs. orbit), and exactly how a changing electric field generates a magnetic field (the displacement current) to allow an electromagnetic wave to detach from an antenna and propagate through space.

1. Why don't positive charges create additional magnetic fields in a magnet?
Quick Resolution: Protons are ~1,836 times heavier than electrons and are locked in a rigid crystal lattice, preventing orbital rotation. Furthermore, the dominant source of permanent magnetism is quantum spin, not orbital motion. While protons do possess spin, their massive size makes their magnetic field ~1,000 times too weak to compete with the electron's field.

1. The Crystal Lattice (Why protons don't orbit)

In a solid permanent magnet (like a block of iron), the positive charges are the heavy atomic nuclei (protons and neutrons). Because protons are about 1,836 times more massive than electrons, they are relatively sluggish. More importantly, in a solid material, these heavy positive nuclei are locked into a rigid crystalline lattice by metallic bonds. They vibrate in place due to thermal energy, but they cannot physically rotate or orbit around the electrons. The electrons, being incredibly light, form a dynamic "cloud" around these fixed positive anchors.

2. The Real Source of Magnetism: Quantum Spin

The classical model of an atom often imagines electrons orbiting the nucleus like planets around a sun, which would constitute a tiny loop of current. While orbital motion contributes a little bit, the dominant source of ferromagnetism (permanent magnets) is actually a quantum property called spin. Electrons possess an intrinsic angular momentum—you can loosely imagine them as tiny, perpetually spinning spheres of negative charge. Because they are charged and spinning, each electron acts like a microscopic bar magnet with its own magnetic dipole moment. When a material becomes magnetized, these electron spins align in the same direction, combining their microscopic fields into a massive macroscopic field.

3. The Nuclear Magnetic Moment (The positive charge's field)

Protons also have quantum spin! This means every proton generates its own tiny magnetic field, known as the nuclear magnetic moment. However, the strength of a particle's magnetic moment (\(\mu\)) is inversely proportional to its mass (\(m\)):

$$\mu \propto \frac{q}{m}$$

Because the mass of a proton is overwhelmingly larger than the mass of an electron, the magnetic moment of the positive nucleus is about 1,000 times weaker than the magnetic moment of the electron. On top of being incredibly weak, the spins of protons in an iron nucleus tend to pair up in opposite directions (up and down), canceling each other out. The electrons in the outer shells of an iron atom, however, have unpaired spins that are free to align and create a strong external field.

2. Why do materials "accept" magnetic fields but "fight" electric fields?
Quick Resolution: Electric fields can only pull positive and negative charges apart, which always generates an opposing internal field. Magnetic fields actually do face opposition from orbital electron motion (diamagnetism), but in magnetic materials, the torque aligning the unpaired electron spins is vastly stronger, overpowering the opposition and "accepting" the field.

1. The Electric Response: Fighting the Field (Dielectric Polarization)

When you apply an external electric field to an insulating material (a dielectric), the material opposes it. Why? Because of the physical separation of charges. Atoms and molecules consist of positive nuclei and negative electron clouds. When an external electric field is applied:

  1. The Pull: The field pulls the positive nuclei in one direction and pushes the negative electron clouds in the exact opposite direction.
  2. The Stretch: The atoms/molecules become microscopic "electric dipoles" (like tiny batteries with a + and - end).
  3. The Opposition: Because these newly formed dipoles have their + and - charges physically pulled apart by the external field, they create their own internal electric field pointing from their + end to their - end. This internal field inevitably points in the exact opposite direction of the external field.

2. The Magnetic Response: Two Competing Battles

Magnetic fields deal with moving charges (currents) and quantum spin. When a magnetic field hits a material, two distinct phenomena happen simultaneously:

A. Diamagnetism (Fighting the Field - Lenz's Law)

  • When an external magnetic field is applied to an atom, the moving electrons in their orbitals experience a change in magnetic flux.
  • According to Lenz's Law, nature hates a change in magnetic flux. The changing flux induces a tiny electromotive force (EMF) that alters the orbital motion of the electrons.
  • These altered orbits create tiny, induced current loops whose sole purpose is to generate a magnetic field that opposes the external field.
  • So, at the pure orbital level, materials do fight magnetic fields. This is diamagnetism, and it happens in all materials.

B. Paramagnetism & Ferromagnetism (Accepting the Field - Spin Alignment)

  • Many atoms have unpaired electrons. These unpaired electrons have intrinsic quantum spin, making them act like permanent, microscopic bar magnets.
  • When an external magnetic field is applied, it exerts a torque on these tiny permanent magnets, physically twisting them to align with the external field.
  • When they align, their individual magnetic fields add to the external field, amplifying it.

Conclusion: In an electric field, pulling + and - charges apart always creates an opposing field. In a magnetic field, the orbital motion (diamagnetism) always fights the field. But, if the atoms have unpaired electron spins, the torque aligning those spins is exponentially stronger than the diamagnetic opposition. The spins align with the field, overpowering the induced opposition.

3. The Link Between Ampère's Law and Complex Integration
Quick Resolution: In two dimensions, Ampère's Law is mathematically identical to Cauchy's Residue Theorem. A current-carrying wire acts as a mathematical singularity (a pole) in a complex plane. The physical current (\(I\)) serves as the "residue."

Step 1: The Physics (The 1/r Magnetic Field)

Imagine an infinitely long wire sitting on the z-axis, carrying current \(I\). We are looking down at the 2D \(xy\)-plane. The magnitude of the magnetic field at a distance \(r\) is \(B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}\).

Since the magnetic field curls in a circle around the wire, its vector direction is \(\hat{\theta}\). In Cartesian coordinates, the full magnetic field vector is:

$$\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi(x^2 + y^2)} (-y\hat{i} + x\hat{j})$$

Setting up the line integral around a closed path (\(d\mathbf{l} = dx\hat{i} + dy\hat{j}\)):

$$\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi} \oint \frac{-y dx + x dy}{x^2 + y^2}$$

Step 2: The Math (The Complex Singularity)

Let's integrate the complex function \(f(z) = 1/z\) around a closed contour enclosing the origin. Expanding \(1/z = \frac{x - iy}{x^2 + y^2}\) and \(dz = dx + i dy\):

$$\oint \frac{1}{z} dz = \oint \left( \frac{x - iy}{x^2 + y^2} \right) (dx + i dy)$$

Separating the real and imaginary parts:

$$\oint \frac{1}{z} dz = \underbrace{\oint \frac{x dx + y dy}{x^2 + y^2}}_{\text{Real}} + i \underbrace{\oint \frac{x dy - y dx}{x^2 + y^2}}_{\text{Imaginary}}$$

Notice the imaginary part is exactly identical to the integral portion of our magnetic field equation from Step 1!

Step 3: Unpacking the Singularity & Cauchy's Theorem

To integrate around the pole without an undefined answer, we parameterize a circular contour using polar coordinates: \(z = R e^{i\theta}\) and \(dz = i R e^{i\theta} d\theta\).

$$\oint \frac{1}{z} dz = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{R e^{i\theta}} (i R e^{i\theta} d\theta)$$

The \(R e^{i\theta}\) terms perfectly cancel out! Leaving:

$$= i \int_0^{2\pi} d\theta = 2\pi i$$

Because the answer is exactly \(0 + i(2\pi)\), the real integral evaluates to \(0\) (physically: magnetic fields have no radial divergence/monopoles), and the imaginary integral evaluates to exactly \(2\pi\).

The Grand Synthesis: Cauchy's theorem states \(\oint f(z) dz = 2\pi i \times (\text{Residue})\). Substitute this mathematical fact (\(2\pi\)) back into the physical Ampère's Law equation:

$$\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi} \times [2\pi] = \mu_0 I$$

The \(2\pi\) generated by the topology of the complex plane perfectly cancels the \(2\pi\) from the radial spread of the magnetic field. The shape of the physical loop doesn't matter as long as the current (the singularity) is enclosed.

4. The Asymmetry of Maxwell's Equations (Ampère vs. Faraday)
Quick Resolution: This asymmetry exists because the universe contains electric charges but lacks magnetic charges (magnetic monopoles). Electric charges can physically flow to create "electric current," providing an extra physical source for magnetic fields. Without magnetic charges, there is no "magnetic current" to provide the equivalent source for electric fields.

The Source of the Asymmetry: No Magnetic Monopoles

The reason the Ampère-Maxwell Law has two parts, while Faraday's Law only has one, boils down to the fact that electric charges exist, but magnetic charges do not.

  • To create a magnetic field (Ampère-Maxwell): You can either physically move electric charges (current, \(I\)) OR you can change an electric field over time.
  • To create an electric field (Faraday): You can only change a magnetic field over time.

Why? Because there is no such thing as a solitary "magnetic charge" (a North pole without a South pole). Because there are no magnetic monopoles, there is no such thing as "magnetic current" (magnetic charges physically flowing through a wire).

The Equations

The Ampère-Maxwell Law (Creating B-Fields): A magnetic field is generated by the total electric current enclosed plus the rate of change of the electric flux.

$$\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{enc} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}$$

Faraday's Law (Creating E-Fields): An electric field is generated only by the rate of change of the magnetic flux. The negative sign represents Lenz's Law.

$$\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = - \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

If a magnetic monopole existed, it would carry a magnetic charge (\(q_m\)). Flowing magnetic charges would create a magnetic current (\(I_m\)). Faraday's Law would then become perfectly symmetrical: \(\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = - \mu_0 I_m - \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}\). Because \(I_m = 0\) in our observable universe, the term vanishes.

5. How a 5G Signal Detaches from an Antenna
Quick Resolution: Without Maxwell's added term, magnetic fields would only exist directly around the physical antenna wire. Maxwell's term dictates that a changing electric field generates a magnetic field out of thin air. This creates a self-sustaining loop allowing the signal to "leapfrog" off the antenna and propagate through free space.

Phase 1: Inside the Metal (The Source)

In a 5G base station, a high-frequency alternating current is pumped into the antenna. Electrons rush up and down the metal rod. This physical current (\(I_{enc}\)) generates an oscillating magnetic field around the wire. Simultaneously, the oscillating separation of charge (top becomes positive, bottom becomes negative, then they flip) creates an oscillating electric field.

Phase 2: The Hand-Off (Leaving the Wire)

As you move away from the physical antenna, the physical current (\(I_{enc}\)) drops to zero because there is no wire in the air. If Ampère's Law only had the current term, the magnetic field would cease to exist. However, the space around the antenna is still filled with the oscillating electric field. Maxwell's displacement current term (\(\mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}\)) takes over, acting as an invisible "current" that continues to generate the magnetic field in empty space.

Phase 3: The Leapfrog (Free Space Propagation)

We now have two laws feeding each other continuously:

  1. The changing electric field creates a changing magnetic field (Ampère-Maxwell Law).
  2. That changing magnetic field instantly creates a new changing electric field (Faraday's Law).

The fields lock into a mutually perpendicular, self-generating loop. They no longer require the physical antenna or the moving electrons. The wave detaches and propagates outward at the speed of light.

6. The Mechanics of an Antenna and Alternating Current
Quick Resolution: A basic antenna is just two pieces of metal. "Alternating Current" (AC) means electrons vibrate back and forth in place. "High frequency" (like 3.5 GHz for 5G) means the transmitter violently pushes and pulls these electrons 3.5 billion times per second.

1. Direct Current (DC) vs. Alternating Current (AC)

DC: Electrons flow continuously through the wire in one direction, like water flowing through a hose.
AC: There is no continuous loop. The transmitter forcefully pushes electrons into one side of the wire, then immediately reverses and sucks them back out. The electrons wiggle back and forth in place. It is like a sealed pipe full of water where a piston rapidly pushes the water left, then right.

2. High Frequency "Sloshing"

Frequency is how fast that back-and-forth wiggle happens. A typical 5G signal operates at around 3.5 GHz. That means the transmitter is violently pumping those electrons up and down the metal rod 3.5 billion times per second.

3. Pumping up and down (Wave Generation)

When the transmitter pushes electrons into the top half of the antenna, the top becomes heavily negatively charged, leaving the bottom half positively charged. Because the electrons physically moved upward to get there, that movement is an electrical current (\(I\)). According to Ampère's Law, this upward current generates a magnetic field wrapping around the rod. A fraction of a nanosecond later, the transmitter reverses. The current is now pointing downward, which instantly flips the direction of the magnetic field. This incredibly violent, fast "sloshing" of charge creates the changing fields that eventually detach as a radio wave.

7. Lenz's Law vs. AI Feedback Loops in 6G
Quick Resolution: Just as nature uses induced currents to maintain magnetic equilibrium (Lenz's Law), modern wireless systems use deep learning loops. When the environment changes, the AI predicts the new channel state and dynamically reconfigures the hardware to maintain signal equilibrium.

The Natural Loop (Lenz's Law)

  1. Perturbation: A magnetic field changes.
  2. Feedback: An electromotive force (EMF) is instantly induced.
  3. Action: Electrons move, creating an opposing magnetic field to cancel the change.

The AI Loop (6G Digital Twins)

  1. Perturbation: A bus drives between the transmitter and receiver, blocking the Line of Sight.
  2. Feedback: The receiver reports a drop in Channel State Information (CSI).
  3. Action: The AI network calculates the loss. Using generative priors for state prediction, it anticipates where the signal can go. It updates the weights on the transmitter's antenna array to instantly steer a new beam off a nearby building (reflection), bypassing the bus and maintaining the link.

The Ultimate Form: Reconfigurable Intelligent Surfaces (RIS)

We are building physical "smart walls" coated in metamaterials. If a comm link drops, the AI can literally send a signal to the wall, changing the electrical properties of the metamaterial to manually bend (refract/reflect) the wave directly to the user. The AI actively forces the environment to move in a way that keeps the communication field constant.

8. Effect-Based Substitution & Ray Tracing Abstractions
Quick Resolution: Brute-forcing billions of rays is computationally impossible. We use mathematical equivalents to trick the physics engine, like abstracting 10 individual rays hitting the same surface into a single ray with scaled amplitude (Beam Tracing).

Abstraction 1: Beam Tracing (Ray Bundling)

Instead of launching 10 infinitely thin rays and calculating 10 separate wall intersections, you launch a single "Beam" (a cone or pyramid shape). When the beam hits a surface, you calculate the intersection once. The amplitude or power of that single hit is scaled based on the cross-sectional area of the beam. This reduces the Python loop complexity by orders of magnitude.

Abstraction 2: Effective Material Properties (BRDF)

When a wave hits a rough brick wall, it physically shatters into thousands of micro-diffractions. Instead of tracing 1,000 scattered rays, we use an effect-based substitution: a Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function (BRDF). We treat the wall as flat, trace one ray, and multiply the amplitude by a probability matrix to represent the scattering loss.

Abstraction 3: Generative Priors

The ultimate abstraction. Instead of running the ray tracing math at all for every minor movement, you abstract the entire physics engine into a neural network. The model instantly hallucinates the correct interference pattern without needing to manually bounce a single ray.

9. The "Delta" Abstraction (Surrogate Models in 6G)
Quick Resolution: Instead of re-tracing an environment when a small change occurs, we use a Residual Network where the input is the small change (delta) and the output is just the adjustment to the previous state. (New Output = Old Output + Delta Output).

The Mathematical Framework

Let the channel state (the ray tracing output) at time \(t\) be \(H_t\). Let the small change in the environment (e.g., a car moving 1 meter) be \(\Delta E\). Instead of calculating a massive function \(f(E_{t+1}) = H_{t+1}\) from scratch, we use a neural network to approximate a residual function:

$$H_{t+1} = H_t + \text{NN}(H_t, \Delta E)$$

Why this is wildly efficient

99% of the environment (the buildings, the transmitter, the static ground) hasn't changed. By predicting only the delta, the neural network requires significantly fewer parameters and computes much faster. It only has to learn the physics of moving objects, not the physics of the entire universe.

Generative Priors

In advanced generative models, \(H_t\) serves as the "prior" distribution. If a link is temporarily blocked, the model conditions its generation on what the channel looked like a millisecond ago. This allows the digital twin to accurately predict the path loss and phase shifts of the new state without doing a single ray-wall intersection calculation.

10. The "Slow Light" Abstraction (Superposition in Dielectrics)
Quick Resolution: Electromagnetic waves (photons) always travel at \(c\). The concept of waves "slowing down" (\(v = c/n\)) in a material is a macroscopic illusion caused by interference.

The Illusion of "Slowing Down"

When the original 5G wave (traveling at \(c\)) enters a brick wall, it hits the atoms and forces the electron clouds to oscillate up and down. Oscillating charges create their own electromagnetic fields. So now you have two waves:

  1. The original wave, traveling at \(c\).
  2. Billions of new, secondary waves generated by the vibrating electrons, also traveling at \(c\), but slightly delayed (out of phase).

When these waves overlap and interfere with each other, they undergo superposition. The resulting combined wave (the macroscopic wave) has a phase velocity that appears to move slower than \(c\). We call this apparent speed \(v\), and we abstract the entire horrifyingly complex quantum interference pattern into a single number: the refractive index (\(n\)).

Why this matters for your code

If you actually tried to code the true physics of a wave propagating through a wall, your computer would crash trying to simulate billions of atomic dipole radiators. Instead, you use the macroscopic abstraction (\(v = c/n\)) and pretend the wave just smoothly "slows down."

Module: Advanced Wave Propagation — Wavefront Architecture, Diffraction & Scattering

Sources: Wave Optics Visualizations (Eugene Khutoryansky) & Wavefront Mechanics Lectures (Bozeman Science)

Module Summary: This module bridges the physical reality of electromagnetic wave propagation and the mathematical abstractions used in ray tracing engines. It covers the geometry of wavefronts, the conditions under which a ray is a valid proxy for energy flow, how diffraction mechanisms bend signals into shadow zones, and how surface micro-structure governs whether a surface reflects specularly or scatters energy diffusely into the environment.

11. Wavefront Mechanics & The Ray Abstraction
Core Concept: A ray is not a physical entity — it is a mathematical construct drawn normal to the wavefront surface, tracking the local direction of Poynting energy flow (\(\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}\)).

Definition: What is a Wavefront?

A wavefront is a locus of points in space sharing an identical phase of an electromagnetic wave — the co-phasal surface where the electric field simultaneously reaches its maximum amplitude. Mathematically, this surface is defined by:

$$\omega t - \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{r} = \text{constant}$$

where \(\omega\) is the angular frequency, \(\mathbf{k}\) is the wave vector, and \(\mathbf{r}\) is the position vector in 3D space.

The Ray Proxy

In geometric ray tracing, a ray is purely a mathematical construct drawn strictly normal (perpendicular) to the physical wavefront surface. Its direction tracks the local vector of Poynting energy flow — the direction in which electromagnetic energy is transported:

$$\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}$$

The ray carries no intrinsic physical width or energy of its own. It is an abstraction of the local propagation direction of the wavefront it represents.

Field Transformations: Near-Field vs. Far-Field

Near-Field (Spherical Wavefronts)

Point sources emit expanding spherical wavefront surfaces. Rays point radially outward (\(\nabla \times \hat{\mathbf{r}} = 0\)). Power density decays via the Inverse Square Law as the fixed total power spreads over an ever-growing spherical surface area:

$$P_d \propto \frac{1}{r^2}$$

Far-Field (Plane Wavefronts)

At vast distances relative to wavelength — specifically where \(r \gg \frac{2D^2}{\lambda}\) (the Fraunhofer distance, with \(D\) being the aperture size) — a localized slice of the spherical wavefront becomes effectively flat. Wavefront surfaces become perfectly parallel planes; the tracking ray vectors align into uniform, non-diverging parallel sets. This is the plane wave approximation that underlies all classical geometric ray tracing.

12. High-Frequency Diffraction (Huygens’ Principle & UTD)
Core Concept: Diffraction bends signal power around sharp obstacles into geometric shadow zones. Computational ray tracers replace full wave integration with the Uniform Theory of Diffraction (UTD) — a localized coefficient and a cone of secondary diffracted rays per edge hit.

Huygens-Fresnel Principle

Every unobstructed point on a primary wavefront acts as a secondary, localized source of spherical wavelets. The forward envelope of all these wavelets synthesizes the next wavefront position. When a wavefront encounters a sharp obstacle edge (e.g., a building corner), it geometrically blocks some secondary sources. The remaining unblocked wavelets continue to expand radially — including inward behind the geometric boundary — bending signal power directly into the "shadow zone" that classical geometric optics would predict to be completely dark.

Frequency Scaling of Diffraction Efficiency

Wavelength \(\lambda\) directly dictates how efficiently edges bend signals:

  • Low frequencies (~700 MHz, \(\lambda \approx 43\text{ cm}\)): Wavelengths are comparable to or larger than urban macro-structures. Diffraction is smooth and efficient — signals bend readily around buildings and terrain obstacles.
  • Next-gen high frequencies (6G sub-THz ~100 GHz, \(\lambda \approx 3\text{ mm}\)): Wavelengths become tiny relative to all structural features. Diffraction efficiency drops to near-zero, casting deep, knife-edge radio shadow zones with sharply defined boundaries and severe signal loss behind them.

Computational Shortcut: Uniform Theory of Diffraction (UTD)

Full wave integration across the aperture is computationally intractable at ray-tracing scale. The UTD provides an efficient geometric approximation by treating diffraction as a local phenomenon at the edge:

  1. Model the physical obstacle edge as a 3D geometric line vector in the scene.
  2. When an incident ray intersects this edge line, compute a localized diffraction coefficient tensor \(\mathbf{D}\) (dependent on geometry, frequency, polarization, and material).
  3. Project a continuous spatial Keller Cone of secondary diffracted rays around the edge axis — all emerging at the same polar angle from the edge as the incident ray, but spread across all azimuthal directions, wrapping around the obstacle corner profile.

This collapses the full aperture integral to a single local calculation per edge intersection, making diffraction tractable in large-scale urban ray tracing simulations.

13. Surface Roughness & Diffuse Scattering (BRDF)
Core Concept: The Rayleigh Criterion determines whether a surface acts as a specular mirror or a diffuse scatterer. For millimeter-wave 6G signals, virtually all common building materials violate the smoothness threshold and shatter specular reflections into diffuse paths.

Rayleigh Roughness Criterion

A physical surface with local protuberances of height \(h\) ceases to behave as a specular mirror and acts as a diffuse wave scatterer when the following threshold is exceeded:

$$h > \frac{\lambda}{8 \cos\theta_i}$$

where \(\theta_i\) is the angle of incidence relative to the surface normal. For millimetric 6G waves (\(\lambda \approx 3\text{ mm}\)), even sub-millimeter surface variations on normal brick, concrete, and masonry elements easily violate this boundary, scattering specular energy into diffuse propagation paths throughout the environment.

BRDF Mathematical Implementations

1. Lambertian Model (Purely Diffuse)

The scattered field power \(P_s\) exiting at any reflection angle \(\theta_s\) depends only on the projection cosine to the surface normal vector — entirely decoupled from the incoming ray direction:

$$P_s(\theta_s) \propto \cos(\theta_s)$$

This is the maximum-entropy scattering model: energy is distributed uniformly across the hemisphere, weighted by the solid angle projection factor. It is physically valid for deeply rough surfaces (concrete, asphalt, bare earth) where all phase memory of the incident wave is destroyed by surface irregularities.

2. Directive Model (Combined Specular + Diffuse)

A more physically accurate model introduces an empirical Scattering Coefficient \(S \in [0, 1]\). Starting from the baseline Fresnel reflection power coefficient \(R\), the specular and diffuse power fractions are apportioned as follows:

  • Specular power fraction: \(R_{\text{specular}} = R \cdot (1 - S)\) — the Fresnel reflection attenuated by the fraction of energy lost to scattering.
  • Diffuse power fraction: \(R \cdot S\) — scales a directive spatial power lobe centered along the geometric specular reflection track, spreading energy around it.

When \(S = 0\), the model degenerates to a pure Fresnel specular mirror. When \(S = 1\), all reflected power is scattered diffusely. Empirically measured values include: glass \(S \approx 0.1\), brick \(S \approx 0.5\text{–}0.7\), rough concrete \(S \approx 0.8\).
